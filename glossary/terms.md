# Diving Practice Handbook - Glossary of Terms

## Purpose

This glossary provides clear definitions of all diving-specific terms used throughout the Diving Practice Handbook. Terms are organized alphabetically for easy reference.

---

## A

**ACA (AI Coding Assistant)**  
An artificial intelligence system used to generate or maintain code for the web application version of this handbook. Must follow strict constraints defined in `build-instructions/aca-constraints.md`.

**Air**  
The standard breathing gas for recreational diving, consisting of approximately 21% oxygen and 79% nitrogen at atmospheric pressure.

**Altitude Diving**  
Diving conducted at elevations significantly above sea level, requiring adjustments to decompression calculations due to reduced atmospheric pressure.

**Ambient Pressure**  
The total pressure surrounding a diver, including both atmospheric pressure and water pressure. Increases by approximately 1 atmosphere for every 10 meters (33 feet) of depth in saltwater.

**Ascent Rate**  
The speed at which a diver returns to the surface, typically measured in meters or feet per minute. Controlled ascent rates (generally 9-10 meters/30 feet per minute or slower) allow safe off-gassing of nitrogen.

**ATM (Atmosphere)**  
A unit of pressure equal to the average atmospheric pressure at sea level (approximately 101,325 Pascals or 14.7 PSI). Used to express pressure underwater.

---

## B

**Backmount**  
A traditional equipment configuration where the scuba cylinder is mounted on the diver's back, secured to a buoyancy control device (BCD).

**Barotrauma**  
Physical injury caused by pressure differences between body air spaces and the surrounding environment. Can affect ears, sinuses, lungs, and other air-containing spaces.

**BCD (Buoyancy Control Device)**  
An inflatable vest or wing worn by divers to control buoyancy underwater by adding or releasing air.

**Bottom Time**  
The total duration from beginning descent to starting ascent on a dive, used in decompression calculations and dive planning.

**Breathing Gas**  
Any gas or gas mixture used for breathing underwater, including air, nitrox, trimix, or heliox.

**Buddy System**  
The fundamental safety practice of diving with a partner who can provide assistance in emergencies and shares responsibility for dive safety.

---

## C

**Carbon Dioxide (CO2)**  
A metabolic waste gas that accumulates during exertion. Inadequate ventilation underwater can lead to dangerous CO2 buildup.

**Certification**  
Formal recognition by a diving training organization (such as PADI, SSI, or CMAS) that a diver has completed required training and demonstrated competency at a specific level.

**CMAS (Confédération Mondiale Des Activités Subaquatiques)**  
World Underwater Federation, an international diving certification and training organization.

**Cold Water Diving**  
Diving in water temperatures typically below 15°C (60°F), requiring specialized thermal protection and equipment considerations.

**Configuration**  
The arrangement and organization of diving equipment on a diver's body, following principles of redundancy, streamlining, and accessibility.

**Conservative Approach**  
A safety-oriented diving philosophy that adds margins beyond minimum requirements, such as ending dives well before no-decompression limits or maintaining larger gas reserves.

**Constitutional Framework**  
The organizational structure of this handbook, designed to maintain internal coherence while allowing controlled evolution of content.

**Current**  
Horizontal water movement that can affect diver navigation, gas consumption, and safety. Requires specific planning and techniques.

---

## D

**DAN (Divers Alert Network)**  
An international organization providing diving emergency services, medical information, and safety research.

**DCS (Decompression Sickness)**  
A condition caused by nitrogen bubbles forming in body tissues when pressure decreases too rapidly during ascent. Also known as "the bends."

**Decompression**  
The process of safely reducing pressure on a diver's body during ascent, allowing dissolved gases to be eliminated without forming harmful bubbles.

**Decompression Stop**  
A mandatory pause at a specific depth during ascent to allow excess nitrogen to safely leave the body before continuing to surface. Required for dives exceeding no-decompression limits.

**Depth**  
The vertical distance below the water surface, typically measured in meters or feet. Directly affects pressure, gas consumption, and decompression requirements.

**Dive Computer**  
An electronic device that monitors depth and time to calculate nitrogen loading and provide real-time decompression information.

**Dive Profile**  
A graphical or tabular representation of a dive showing depth versus time, used for planning and analyzing dives.

**Dive Tables**  
Printed charts used to plan dives and calculate no-decompression limits, surface intervals, and repetitive dive procedures based on depth and time.

**Drysuit**  
A waterproof suit that keeps the diver dry by sealing at wrists, neck, and sometimes feet, providing thermal protection through insulation layers worn underneath.

---

## E

**Environmental Stressor**  
Any environmental factor that increases physical or psychological demands on a diver, such as cold, current, poor visibility, or altitude.

**Equipment Redundancy**  
The practice of carrying backup systems for critical functions (such as alternate air sources, backup lights, or multiple cutting tools) to ensure safety if primary equipment fails.

---

## F

**Freediving**  
Diving on a single breath of air without scuba equipment, requiring different techniques and safety considerations.

**FRC (Freediving Recovery Check)**  
A safety assessment performed after breath-hold diving to ensure the diver has recovered adequately before attempting another dive.

---

## G

**Gas Mixture**  
The specific composition of gases (such as oxygen, nitrogen, and helium) in a diver's breathing supply, expressed as percentages by volume.

**Gas Laws**  
Physical principles (such as Boyle's Law, Dalton's Law, and Henry's Law) that describe how gases behave under changing pressure and temperature, fundamental to understanding diving physiology.

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)**  
European Union regulation governing data privacy and protection, which applies to any web application collecting user information, including digital versions of this handbook.

**Go/No-Go Decision**  
A structured decision-making process used before dives to evaluate whether conditions, equipment, and team readiness meet safety criteria for proceeding.

---

## H

**Heliox**  
A breathing gas mixture of helium and oxygen, used in commercial diving or extreme depth diving to reduce narcosis and breathing resistance.

**Helium (He)**  
An inert gas sometimes used in technical diving gas mixtures to reduce nitrogen narcosis at depth. Does not cause narcosis but increases heat loss and requires special decompression considerations.

**Human Error**  
Mistakes or lapses in judgment that can occur during diving, categorized as slips (execution errors), mistakes (decision errors), or violations (intentional rule-breaking).

**Hypothermia**  
Dangerously low body core temperature resulting from prolonged exposure to cold water, impairing physical and mental function.

---

## I

**Incident Analysis**  
Systematic review of diving accidents or near-misses to identify root causes, contributing factors, and preventive measures.

**Instructor**  
A certified diving professional authorized to teach and certify students in diving skills and knowledge.

---

## L

**Lake Diving**  
Diving in freshwater lakes, which may involve unique challenges such as reduced buoyancy compared to seawater, thermoclines, and limited visibility.

---

## N

**N2 (Nitrogen)**  
The primary inert gas in air (approximately 79%), which dissolves in body tissues under pressure and must be safely eliminated during ascent to prevent decompression sickness.

**Narcosis**  
See Nitrogen Narcosis.

**NDL (No-Decompression Limit)**  
The maximum time a diver can spend at a given depth and still ascend directly to the surface (with safety stop) without requiring mandatory decompression stops.

**Nitrox (Enriched Air Nitrox, EAN)**  
A breathing gas mixture with higher oxygen content than normal air (typically 32% or 36% oxygen), which reduces nitrogen loading and extends no-decompression limits but requires oxygen toxicity management.

**Nitrogen Loading**  
The accumulation of dissolved nitrogen in body tissues during a dive, which must be carefully managed to prevent decompression sickness.

**Nitrogen Narcosis**  
An impairment of mental and physical performance caused by breathing nitrogen under increased pressure at depth, similar to alcohol intoxication. Generally becomes noticeable below 30 meters (100 feet) on air.

---

## O

**O2 (Oxygen)**  
Essential gas for human respiration, comprising approximately 21% of air. While necessary for life, oxygen becomes toxic at elevated partial pressures encountered in diving.

**Off-Gassing**  
The process by which dissolved inert gases (primarily nitrogen) are eliminated from body tissues during ascent or at the surface after a dive.

**Oxygen Toxicity**  
Harmful physiological effects caused by breathing oxygen at high partial pressures, manifesting as central nervous system toxicity (potentially causing convulsions) or pulmonary toxicity (lung irritation from prolonged exposure).

---

## P

**PADI (Professional Association of Diving Instructors)**  
One of the world's largest recreational diving training organizations, offering certification programs at multiple levels.

**Partial Pressure**  
The pressure exerted by a single gas component in a mixture, calculated by multiplying the gas fraction by the total (ambient) pressure. Critical for managing oxygen toxicity and nitrogen narcosis.

**Post-Dive Analysis**  
Systematic reflection after diving to review decisions, equipment performance, team coordination, and identify learning opportunities for continuous improvement.

**PPO2 (Partial Pressure of Oxygen)**  
The pressure exerted specifically by oxygen in a breathing gas mixture. Must be monitored to avoid oxygen toxicity, typically kept below 1.4 bar for recreational diving and 1.6 bar for decompression.

**Pressure**  
Force per unit area, measured in various units including bar, atmospheres (ATM), or pounds per square inch (PSI). Increases linearly with depth underwater.

**Pressure Physics**  
The study of how pressure changes underwater affect gas volumes, gas behavior in the body, buoyancy, and equipment function.

**PSI (Pounds per Square Inch)**  
A unit of pressure commonly used to measure cylinder pressure in diving, particularly in countries using imperial measurements.

---

## R

**RDP (Recreational Dive Planner)**  
PADI's dive table system for planning no-decompression dives and calculating surface intervals.

**Recreational Diving**  
Diving conducted for enjoyment within the limits of no-decompression diving, typically to maximum depths of 40 meters (130 feet) with appropriate certification.

**Redundancy**  
Backup equipment or systems that can take over if primary systems fail, essential for managing single-point failure risks in diving. See Equipment Redundancy.

**Regulator**  
The device that reduces high-pressure gas from the cylinder to ambient pressure for breathing underwater.

**Residual Nitrogen**  
Nitrogen remaining in body tissues from previous dives, which must be accounted for when planning repetitive dives.

**Risk Assessment**  
Systematic evaluation of potential hazards, their probability, and potential consequences to make informed decisions about dive safety.

**Risk Matrix**  
A tool for visualizing and quantifying diving risks by plotting probability against severity, used to prioritize safety measures and inform Go/No-Go decisions.

---

## S

**Safety Stop**  
A precautionary pause at approximately 5 meters (15 feet) for 3-5 minutes before surfacing, recommended even on no-decompression dives to provide additional margin for off-gassing.

**SAC Rate (Surface Air Consumption)**  
A measurement of how much gas a diver consumes at the surface (normalized for depth), expressed in liters or cubic feet per minute. Used for gas planning.

**SCUBA (Self-Contained Underwater Breathing Apparatus)**  
Equipment allowing divers to breathe underwater using compressed gas carried in cylinders.

**Self-Awareness**  
Conscious monitoring of one's physical state, psychological condition, and environmental surroundings during diving. Identified as the cornerstone of safe diving practice in this handbook.

**Self-Reflection**  
The practice of analyzing one's decisions, actions, and performance after each dive to identify learning opportunities and areas for improvement.

**Side-Mount**  
An equipment configuration where cylinders are carried on the diver's sides rather than on the back, often used for technical or cave diving.

**Situational Assessment**  
Continuous evaluation of environmental conditions, personal readiness, equipment status, and team coordination to inform decision-making during diving.

**SSI (Scuba Schools International)**  
A major international diving certification and training organization offering programs at multiple skill levels.

**Streamlining**  
Organizing equipment to minimize drag and reduce entanglement hazards, improving efficiency and safety underwater.

**Stress**  
Physical or psychological pressure that can impair judgment and performance. Chapter 5 addresses stress recognition and management in diving.

**Surface Interval**  
The time spent at the surface between repetitive dives, during which nitrogen continues to off-gas from tissues. Must be factored into dive planning.

---

## T

**Task Loading**  
The cognitive and physical demands placed on a diver at any given moment. Excessive task loading increases error probability and stress.

**Team Diving**  
Diving with one or more partners using coordinated communication, shared responsibility, and mutual support following established protocols.

**Technical Diving**  
Advanced diving beyond recreational limits, which may involve decompression obligations, overhead environments, depths beyond 40 meters, or specialized gas mixtures.

**Thermal Protection**  
Equipment (wetsuits, drysuits, hoods, gloves) worn to prevent heat loss and hypothermia during diving.

**Thermocline**  
A layer in water where temperature changes rapidly with depth, which can affect buoyancy control and diver comfort.

**Tissue**  
In decompression theory, refers to different body compartments that absorb and release nitrogen at different rates, represented in dive computers and tables as "fast" and "slow" tissues.

**Trimix**  
A breathing gas mixture containing oxygen, nitrogen, and helium, used in technical diving to manage both oxygen toxicity and nitrogen narcosis at significant depths.

---

## U

**Underwater Navigation**  
Techniques for maintaining orientation and reaching intended destinations while submerged, using natural references, compasses, or other tools.

---

## V

**Visibility**  
The distance a diver can see clearly underwater, affected by water clarity, particles, lighting, and conditions. Poor visibility increases disorientation risk and stress.

**Violation**  
In human error terminology, a deliberate deviation from established procedures or rules, as distinct from unintentional mistakes or slips.

---

## W

**Warm Water Diving**  
Diving in water temperatures typically above 24°C (75°F), requiring less thermal protection but attention to hydration and sun exposure.

**Wetsuit**  
A thermal protection garment made of neoprene that provides insulation by trapping a thin layer of water against the skin, which is warmed by body heat.
